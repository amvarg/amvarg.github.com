I learned to keep comfort-to-reach and hard-to-reach areas in mind when designing. Sliders are also a useful tool when trying to display additional content that may not fit within the display window. Best Practices
For Modals

Modals, or an element that sits on top of an application’s main window, are usually dreaded by users. Most people automatically dismiss these windows. However, they can be useful when grabbing the users attention, getting user input, showing additional information in context, or showing independent, unrelated information.

However, you do not want to use a modal to show error, success, or warning messages — keep those on the page. Modals also need an escape, whether it is a cancel button, close button, escape key, or clicking outside of the window. This escape must also be keyboard accessible, and keep low-vision users in mind. Modal titles must be descriptive, along with buttons. These names should be actionable, and understandable. Modal sizing must also be just right, the content should fit the modal, and shouldn’t take up the entire screen.

A lightbox effect (darkening of the background) should be used when opening a modal, and the user should trigger this. Modals should not randomly pop up, since users will get surprised and close it quickly. Also, another thing to keep in mind is that modals don’t usually work well on mobile devices.

Best Practices
For Form Design

After reading Best practices for form design by Salim Ansari, I felt like I had a much better understanding of usability in the context of web forms.

When it comes to creating content for a form, make sure you are only asking for the most relevant information. Also try and format the form in one column - if needed, chunking the form into several, labeled steps may increase the likelihood of form completion. Asking complex questions (like credit card information, or social security numbers) should be done towards the end of the form. It is also important to clarify formatting or input requirements, as the user may abandon the form due to lack of instructions. I also learned to avoid generic labels on submission/action buttons, and to disable the action button after a tap so there is no duplicate submission. Post-submission feedback is also quite useful.

These are just some examples of the tips I learned during the reading. This content will be especially useful as we move forward in the class, and complete our Mad Libs 2.0 project! I am also very excited to start thinking some more about accessibility, and user experience in future working during this class.

A form that I have interacted with fairly recently is the Reddit.com sign up form. It is very simple. The user can either sign up using google or their apple id, or create an account from scratch through email. The action buttons are clearly labeled, and stand out very well. The user is also given feedback throughout the process. Required fields are made obvious, and there is support text wherever necessary. Although it is a very short, two step process, it is well done. The simplicity, I’m sure, helps with form completion.
